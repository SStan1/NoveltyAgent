## 1. Paper Content Summary

The main objective of this paper is to introduce AgentClinic, a multimodal agent benchmark designed to evaluate Large Language Models (LLMs) in simulated, interactive clinical environments. The research addresses the core limitations of existing medical AI evaluations, which predominantly rely on static question-answering formats that fail to capture the complex, sequential decision-making and incomplete information inherent in real-world clinical practice. To solve this, the authors develop a dialogue-driven simulation utilizing four distinct language agents: a doctor (the model under evaluation), a patient, a measurement agent for providing physical exams and multimodal imaging results, and a moderator for assessing diagnosis accuracy. The benchmark incorporates realistic case studies drawn from electronic health records and medical licensing exams, spanning nine medical specialties and seven languages. Furthermore, the methodology tests models under 24 cognitive and implicit biases and evaluates their proficiency with tools like adaptive Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)—a method for dynamically retrieving external medical information—and persistent memory notebooks. The findings reveal that sequential clinical diagnosis is significantly more challenging than static answering, with Claude-3.5 generally outperforming other models. Additionally, the study demonstrates that tool integration can vastly improve performance (e.g., Llama-3 saw up to a 92% relative improvement using the notebook tool), while the introduction of clinical biases negatively impacts both diagnostic accuracy and novel patient-centric metrics like treatment compliance and consultation confidence.

## 2. Point-wise Novelty Analysis

### 2.1. Novelty Point 1: (Classification: Classification: System/Infrastructure)

**a) Claimed Novelty:**
Classification: Classification: System/Infrastructure.

Explanation: The AgentClinic framework is a System/Infrastructure novelty designed to evaluate the sequential decision-making capabilities of artificial intelligence in a clinical context, moving beyond the limitations of static question-answering (QA) benchmarks. It implements a multi-agent simulation environment featuring four specialized language agents: a doctor, a patient, a measurement instrument, and a moderator. This architecture allows the "doctor" agent to engage in natural dialogue with the "patient," order medical examinations via the "measurement instrument," and process multimodal data to resolve diagnostic uncertainty. The technical contribution is supported by an empirical benchmark of seven large language models (LLMs), which demonstrates that interactive diagnostic accuracy is significantly lower than performance on static medical exams, suggesting that AgentClinic provides a more realistic and rigorous assessment of an AI's clinical utility.

**b) Similarities:**
The use of a multi-agent simulation to facilitate medical dialogue is also explored in the AMIE (Articulate Medical Intelligence Explorer) system, which utilizes a self-play environment consisting of a "Doctor Agent," a "Patient Agent," and a "Moderator" to scale learning across medical conditions [1]. Both AgentClinic and AMIE aim to move beyond single-turn interactions like question-answering or summarization toward more complex, multi-turn diagnostic dialogues [1]. Furthermore, the "role-playing" framework introduced by CAMEL shares the underlying mechanism of using inception prompting to allow autonomous agents to complete tasks through collaborative conversation, similar to the interaction between the doctor and patient agents in AgentClinic [2]. The concept of specialized medical agents mimicking different clinical roles (e.g., radiologists or pathologists) to form a cohesive medical opinion is also discussed as a promising future direction in existing medical LLM surveys [3].

**c) Unique Differences:**
The primary distinction of AgentClinic is its specific four-agent configuration, particularly the inclusion of a "measurement instrument" agent to facilitate the ordering and delivery of medical exams and multimodal data within the interactive loop. While existing systems like AMIE evaluate axes such as "Investigation Appropriate Recommended," the simulation described in AMIE primarily focuses on the dialogue between the doctor, patient, and a critic/moderator for training and refinement [1]. AgentClinic uniquely formalizes the "measurement instrument" as a distinct interacting entity to simulate the acquisition of objective medical data. Additionally, AgentClinic is positioned specifically as a benchmark to reveal the performance gap between static and sequential decision-making; it provides evidence that LLM diagnostic accuracy drops in interactive settings, a finding that contrasts with the typical focus of existing medical AI research on matching or exceeding human performance in static or fixed-input tasks [1] [4].

**d) Details of Unique Differences:**
As a System/Infrastructure innovation, AgentClinic addresses the real-world problem that high scores on static medical benchmarks (like the USMLE) do not necessarily translate to clinical competence in interactive, uncertain environments. Its design uniquely addresses this by partitioning the clinical encounter into four distinct roles, ensuring that the doctor agent must actively "pull" information from both the patient (subjective) and the measurement instrument (objective) agents. The system's practicality and benefit are demonstrated through a benchmarking study of seven major LLMs, which provides the new insight that interactive diagnostic accuracy is a more difficult metric to satisfy than static accuracy. This validates the framework as a more rigorous testing ground for AI safety and efficacy before real-world deployment, filling a gap in existing evaluative resources that rely on fixed datasets [1] [4].

### 2.2. Novelty Point 2: (Classification: Classification: Dataset/Benchmark)

**a) Claimed Novelty:**
Classification: Classification: Dataset/Benchmark.

Explanation: The novelty of AgentClinic lies in its creation of a comprehensive, multimodal benchmark designed to evaluate AI agents within simulated clinical environments. Technically, it structures its cases by leveraging real-world electronic health records (EHRs) and multimodal medical journal challenges, covering nine distinct medical specialties and seven different languages. This methodology moves beyond static question-answering by requiring agents to engage in interactive diagnostic tasks, such as requesting and interpreting physical examination findings and medical imaging. The benchmark aims to address existing gaps in linguistic diversity and realism, providing a platform to study how language models generalize across specialized domains and language barriers. Its utility is supported by a clinical reader study where human doctors assessed the realism, empathy, and accuracy of the generated dialogues.

**b) Similarities:**
Based on the retrieved related texts, several similarities exist regarding the use of simulated environments and evaluation frameworks. Like AgentClinic, the AMIE system utilizes a self-play based simulated learning environment to scale medical knowledge across various conditions and contexts [5]. Similarly, the HELM framework advocates for a multi-metric perspective that includes accuracy, fairness, and bias, arguing that these desiderata should be evaluated in the same contexts where models are deployed [6]. Furthermore, the use of LLM-based agents to simulate social interactions and decision-making is established in frameworks like CAMEL and MetaAgents, which utilize role-playing and multi-turn conversations to explore agent behaviors [7] [8]. Existing work also identifies that LLM evaluators can suffer from inherent biases, such as self-preference or narcissistic evaluation, which can skew scoring [9] [10].

**c) Unique Differences:**
The primary distinction is the empirical focus on the specific relationship between *injected* clinical biases and the degradation of patient-centric outcomes (trust and compliance) relative to stable diagnostic accuracy. While AMIE (REF_010) measures communication and trust, it focuses on comparing AI performance against human physicians in a randomized, double-blind crossover study of text-based consultations designed in the style of an OSCE rather than systematically perturbing the agents with a library of 24 specific cognitive and implicit biases to observe their isolated effects [11]. Unlike the HELM framework, which advocates for evaluating metrics like bias and toxicity across the same core scenarios used to measure accuracy [6], or Galactica, which uses a curated scientific corpus to avoid mirroring the misinformation and stereotypes found in general-purpose models [12], this novelty point describes a dynamic analytical finding: that bias acts as a specific catalyst for losing patient trust and compliance even when the "correct" medical answer is reached. This specifically addresses the "accuracy-trust gap" in a way that prior benchmarks, which largely focus on accuracy or static bias detection, do not explore.

**d) Details of Unique Differences:**
The central new insight is that diagnostic accuracy is an insufficient proxy for effective healthcare delivery because it can remain high while interpersonal metrics like trust and compliance collapse due to implicit biases. This finding challenges the existing belief that a "correct" diagnosis is the most vital metric for medical AI. The validity of this insight is supported by an experimental design that uses system prompt engineering to inject 24 specific biases into doctor and patient agents, allowing for a controlled comparison between objective diagnostic correctness and subjective quantitative rating scales from the patient agent’s perspective. By demonstrating this divergence, the analysis highlights a critical requirement for medical AI: the need for alignment that preserves the patient-provider relationship, not just the accuracy of the medical output.

### 2.4. Novelty Point 4: (Classification: Classification: Empirical/Analytical)

**a) Claimed Novelty:**
Classification: Classification: Empirical/Analytical.

Explanation: This novelty point identifies a significant empirical finding regarding the non-uniform impact of tool-augmented reasoning—specifically "persistent experiential learning notebooks"—on the clinical diagnostic performance of various large language models (LLMs). The methodology involves providing an agent with an editable notebook that persists across sequential patient cases, allowing the model to record and accumulate experiential knowledge to mitigate deficits in sequential decision-making. The central discovery is that the utility of such tools is highly model-dependent: while some models like Claude 3.5 reach peak performance and others like Llama 3 exhibit massive relative accuracy gains (up to 92%), others, specifically GPT-3.5, suffer performance degradation when integrated with the same tools. This finding is supported by an ablation study across six different agent tools and multiple LLM backbones, challenging the prevailing assumption in AI research that external tools like adaptive retrieval or reflection modules provide a universal benefit to model reasoning capabilities.

**b) Similarities:**
The concept of using a persistent memory structure to accumulate experiential knowledge is explored in several existing works. For instance, the ExpeL agent autonomously gathers experiences and extracts knowledge in natural language from training tasks to inform future decisions without parametric updates [13]. Similarly, the "Generative Agents" architecture utilizes a "memory stream" to maintain a complete record of an agent's experiences, which are then synthesized into higher-level "reflections" to guide behavior [14]. In the domain of embodied agents, VOYAGER employs an "ever-growing skill library" of executable code to store and retrieve complex behaviors across different sessions [15]. Furthermore, the use of iterative reasoning chains to improve diagnostic accuracy is shared with AMIE, which employs a three-step chain-of-reasoning (analyzing, formulating, and refining) to arrive at a grounded reply in a clinical context [11].

**c) Unique Differences:**
The primary distinction is this work's Analytical/Empirical focus on the **divergent performance outcomes** across different LLM backbones when using identical tool-augmented reasoning structures. While existing works like ExpeL and Generative Agents generally report consistent performance improvements or define their architectures as "critical to strong performance" [13], [14], this novelty point explicitly identifies a "performance drop" in specific models like GPT-3.5 when tools are introduced. Unlike ToolLLM, which focuses on the difficulty of mastering 16,000+ APIs and general tool-use frameworks [16], this analysis isolates the "persistent notebook" as a specific variable within a clinical agentic loop to measure relative accuracy shifts. It provides a comparative benchmark (e.g., Llama 3's 92% improvement vs. GPT-3.5's decrease) that challenges the assumption of uniform tool benefit.

**d) Details of Unique Differences:**
The central new insight is that tool-augmented reasoning does not act as a universal performance booster but instead interacts with a model's underlying architecture in ways that can either dramatically enhance or actively hinder clinical reasoning. This finding challenges the belief that providing more "reasoning scaffolding," such as persistent notebooks or reflection tools, will inherently improve outcomes in complex tasks. The validity of this insight is supported by a comprehensive ablation study that evaluated six distinct agent tools across multiple backbones, revealing that while a model like Llama 3 can overcome significant sequential decision-making deficits to achieve a 92 percent relative accuracy gain, the additional complexity of managing these tools causes GPT-3.5's performance to degenerate. This suggests that the "cognitive overhead" or instruction-following limitations of certain models may make tool integration counterproductive, a nuanced finding that informs future agent design by emphasizing the need for model-specific tool optimization.

### 2.5. Novelty Point 5: (Classification: Classification: Empirical/Analytical)

**a) Claimed Novelty:**
Classification: Classification: Empirical/Analytical.

Explanation: This novelty point presents an empirical and analytical finding that a large language model's (LLM) performance on static medical question-answering (QA) benchmarks, such as MedQA or USMLE-style exams, is a poor predictor of its diagnostic accuracy in dynamic, interactive clinical simulations. The methodology involves a comparative analysis of seven major language models, demonstrating that solving medical problems in a dialogue-driven, sequential decision-making environment is significantly more complex than selecting answers in static multiple-choice formats. The evidence shows that diagnostic accuracies in these interactive settings drop to a fraction of the scores achieved on static benchmarks, and there is only a weak correlation between the two performance metrics, especially when interaction time or the number of inference steps is constrained. This finding challenges the current research reliance on static benchmarks to determine if an AI is "clinically ready," arguing instead that interactive environments are essential for a true assessment of medical capability.

**b) Similarities:**
The challenge of transitioning from static medical knowledge to interactive dialogue is acknowledged in existing research, which notes that medical diagnostic dialogue is a particularly difficult use case due to the complexity of interactive information gathering [1]. Existing work also recognizes that while LLMs have proven capable of highly accurate single-turn medical question-answering, their conversational capabilities for history-taking require more rigorous examination [1]. Furthermore, prior studies have observed that AI models interpreting fixed clinical inputs perform differently than those required to actively acquire information through conversation under uncertainty [1]. Some research has also pointed out that even high-performing models on medical benchmarks like MedQA-USMLE still face limitations in real-world reasoning and expert domain knowledge application [4]. Additionally, earlier attempts to create models for simulated medical conversations demonstrated that accuracy in answer selection remained limited, suggesting the inherent difficulty of the interactive task [17].

**c) Unique Differences:**
The primary distinction is the empirical and analytical focus on the lack of correlation between static and interactive performance across a broad spectrum of seven major LLMs. While existing research like the AMIE study focuses on optimizing a specific model for dialogue and compares it to human physicians, this novelty point provides a systemic critique of static benchmarks themselves by quantifying their failure to predict interactive success [1]. It introduces a specific analytical finding: that diagnostic accuracy is not just lower, but falls to a "fraction" of static scores, and that this performance is poorly correlated across multiple different model architectures. Unlike previous works that aim to improve benchmark scores or develop specific conversational agents, this analysis specifically investigates how constraints on interaction time and inference steps further decouple static knowledge from interactive diagnostic utility [4] [1].

**d) Details of Unique Differences:**
The central new finding is the identification of a significant "capability gap" where high performance on medical board exams does not translate into effective clinical dialogue, effectively challenging the prevailing belief that static benchmarks are a valid proxy for clinical readiness [4] [1]. This insight is supported by an experimental design that tests seven distinct LLMs on both standard medical QA datasets and a new interactive benchmark, revealing that the correlation between the two is weak. The validity of this finding is further established by demonstrating that when the number of dialogue turns or inference steps is limited, the models' ability to reach an accurate diagnosis collapses, even if their underlying medical knowledge (as measured by static tests) remains high. This suggests that the procedural skill of information acquisition through sequential decision-making is a distinct capability from the retrieval of medical facts, necessitating a shift in how the medical AI community evaluates model performance [1].


## 3. Novelty Summary

This paper demonstrates an integration-driven approach and significant empirical breakthroughs by shifting the evaluation of medical artificial intelligence from static question-answering to dynamic, interactive clinical simulations. By combining a novel multi-agent architecture with a diverse, multimodal dataset and rigorous behavioral analyses, the work systematically exposes the limitations of current evaluation paradigms. It challenges prevailing assumptions by proving that static medical knowledge does not equate to interactive clinical competence, while highlighting critical, previously under-researched vulnerabilities regarding patient trust and the non-uniform impact of tool-augmented reasoning. 

*   **Genuine Innovations:** The most significant genuine innovation is the formalization of a four-agent clinical simulation that uniquely introduces a "measurement instrument" agent alongside the doctor, patient, and moderator. This technically forces the primary diagnostic agent to actively request and interpret objective multimodal data across diverse languages and medical specialties, moving beyond text-centric, single-turn interactions to rigorously test sequential decision-making under uncertainty. Another major contribution is the empirical discovery of the "accuracy-trust gap." By systematically injecting 24 specific cognitive and implicit biases into the agents, the authors demonstrate that interpersonal metrics like patient trust and treatment compliance can severely degrade even when objective diagnostic accuracy remains stable, proving that accuracy alone is an insufficient proxy for safe healthcare delivery. Furthermore, the paper provides highly novel analytical insights into model behaviors, specifically quantifying that tool-augmented reasoning (such as persistent experiential notebooks) yields divergent, model-dependent results—causing significant performance drops in certain models rather than providing universal benefits. Finally, it systemically proves that high performance on static medical exams is a poor, weakly correlated predictor of interactive diagnostic success, establishing a new imperative for dynamic evaluation.

*   **Aspects Lacking Originality:** The foundational use of multi-agent role-playing (e.g., doctor, patient, and moderator agents) and standard clinical evaluation rubrics (assessing realism, accuracy, and empathy) represents a **Standard Practice** widely utilized in prior simulated medical environments and conversational agent frameworks. *Improvement suggestion:* The authors could develop entirely new, interaction-specific evaluation metrics tailored to the unique dynamics of the measurement instrument rather than relying on conventional clinical examination rubrics. Additionally, the implementation of persistent memory structures or experiential learning notebooks to accumulate knowledge across sessions is a **Direct Repetition** of established agentic memory architectures. *Improvement suggestion:* Future iterations should propose a novel memory mechanism specifically optimized for clinical reasoning and medical evidence weighting, rather than applying generalized persistent notebooks.

**Final One-line Summary:**  
**3 – Good / Significant:** This work achieves clear and substantial progress by introducing a well-motivated interactive evaluation framework and presenting substantial new empirical insights that challenge the community's reliance on static benchmarks and universal tool benefits in medical AI.
