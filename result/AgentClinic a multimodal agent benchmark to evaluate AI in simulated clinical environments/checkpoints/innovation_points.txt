1. (Classification: System/Infrastructure) A multi-agent software framework named AgentClinic for simulating interactive clinical environments to evaluate sequential medical decision-making; HOW: The system addresses the real-world problem that existing medical artificial intelligence evaluations rely on static question-answering, which fails to capture the multiplexed, sequential nature of clinical work. It uniquely addresses this by deploying four distinct interacting language agents representing a doctor, a patient, a measurement instrument, and a moderator, allowing the doctor agent to dynamically collect multimodal data, order medical exams, and handle diagnostic uncertainty through natural dialogue; Evidence: Practicality and benefit are demonstrated by benchmarking seven major large language models within the framework, revealing that diagnostic accuracy in this interactive format drops significantly compared to static evaluations, thereby providing a more rigorous and realistic test of clinical utility.

2. (Classification: Dataset/Benchmark) A comprehensive suite of simulated clinical case datasets spanning diverse medical specialties, languages, and real-world electronic health records; HOW: This benchmark fills the gap of limited diversity, realism, and multimodal support in existing conversational medical evaluations by structuring cases from real electronic health records, multimodal medical journal challenges, nine distinct medical specialties, and seven different languages. This enables new research questions regarding how well language models generalize across linguistic barriers, specialized medical domains, and complex multimodal diagnostic tasks where image readings and physical examination findings must be requested and interpreted; Evidence: The benchmark's utility is validated through extensive evaluations of models across hundreds of cases, alongside a clinical reader study involving human medical doctors who rated the generated patient-doctor dialogues for realism, measurement accuracy, and empathy.

3. (Classification: Empirical/Analytical) The insight that simulated cognitive and implicit biases significantly degrade patient-centric metrics like trust and compliance, even when diagnostic accuracy remains stable; HOW: The central insight is that while highly aligned models can maintain diagnostic accuracy when perturbed with biases, these biases cause simulated patients to report drastically lower confidence in the doctor, reduced likelihood of treatment compliance, and lower willingness for follow-up consultations. This challenges the prior belief that diagnostic accuracy alone is a sufficient metric for evaluating medical artificial intelligence, highlighting the importance of interpersonal and bias-related factors in healthcare delivery; Evidence: The experimental design supports this validity by systematically injecting 24 specific cognitive and implicit biases into the system prompts of patient and doctor agents, and then measuring both the objective diagnosis correctness and subjective quantitative rating scales from the patient agent's perspective.

4. (Classification: Empirical/Analytical) The discovery that the integration of specific agent tools, particularly persistent experiential learning notebooks, yields vastly different performance benefits across different language models; HOW: The central insight is that while baseline models struggle with sequential clinical reasoning, providing tools like an editable notebook that persists across different patient cases allows models to accumulate experiential knowledge, leading to massive relative accuracy improvements for certain models while causing performance drops in others. This confirms that tool-augmented reasoning can mitigate sequential decision-making deficits but challenges the assumption that all models benefit uniformly from external tools like adaptive retrieval or reflection; Evidence: Validity is established through an ablation study evaluating six different agent tools across multiple language model backbones, demonstrating that while models like Claude 3.5 achieve peak accuracy with the notebook tool, others like Llama 3 show up to a 92 percent relative improvement, and models like GPT-3.5 experience decreased performance across all tools.

5. (Classification: Empirical/Analytical) The empirical finding that a language model's performance on static medical question-answering benchmarks is poorly predictive of its diagnostic accuracy in interactive clinical simulations; HOW: The central insight is that solving medical problems in a dialogue-driven, sequential decision-making format is considerably more challenging than static multiple-choice evaluations, causing diagnostic accuracies to drop to a fraction of their original scores. This challenges the prevailing reliance on static benchmarks to gauge the clinical readiness of large language models, suggesting that interactive environments are necessary for true capability assessment; Evidence: The finding is supported by comparative experiments showing that the accuracy of seven major language models on a standard medical question-answering dataset has only a weak correlation with their performance on the interactive benchmark, with absolute diagnostic accuracy dropping significantly when the interaction time or the number of inference steps is constrained.
