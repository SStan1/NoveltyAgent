## 1. Paper Content Summary

This paper introduces AgentClinic, a multimodal agent benchmark designed to evaluate Large Language Models (LLMs) within simulated, interactive clinical environments. The research addresses the primary limitations of existing medical AI evaluations, which rely heavily on static question-answering formats that fail to capture the complex, sequential decision-making and incomplete information inherent in real-world clinical practice. To address this, the authors developed a dialogue-driven simulation utilizing four distinct language agents: a doctor (the model under evaluation), a patient, a measurement agent (providing physical exams and multimodal imaging results), and a moderator (assessing diagnostic accuracy). The benchmark incorporates realistic case studies derived from electronic health records and medical licensing exams, spanning nine medical specialties and seven languages. Furthermore, the methodology tests models under 24 cognitive and implicit biases and evaluates their proficiency with tools such as adaptive Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and persistent memory notebooks. The findings reveal that sequential clinical diagnosis is significantly more challenging than static answering, with Claude-3.5 generally outperforming other models. Additionally, the study demonstrates that tool integration can vastly improve performance—for example, Llama-3 achieved a 92% relative improvement using the notebook tool—while the introduction of clinical biases negatively impacts both diagnostic accuracy and patient-centric metrics, such as treatment compliance and consultation confidence.

## 2. Point-wise Novelty Analysis

### 2.1. Novelty Point 1: (Classification: System/Infrastructure)

**a) Claimed Novelty:**
Classification: System/Infrastructure.

Explanation: The AgentClinic framework is a System/Infrastructure novelty designed to evaluate the sequential decision-making capabilities of AI in a clinical context, moving beyond the limitations of static question-answering (QA) benchmarks. It implements a multi-agent simulation environment featuring four specialized language agents: a doctor, a patient, a measurement instrument, and a moderator. This architecture allows the "doctor" agent to engage in natural dialogue with the "patient," order medical examinations via the "measurement instrument," and process multimodal data to resolve diagnostic uncertainty. This technical contribution is supported by an empirical benchmark of seven large language models (LLMs), demonstrating that interactive diagnostic accuracy is significantly lower than performance on static medical exams. This suggests that AgentClinic provides a more realistic and rigorous assessment of an AI's clinical utility.

**b) Similarities:**
The use of multi-agent simulations to facilitate medical dialogue is also explored in the AMIE (Articulate Medical Intelligence Explorer) system, which utilizes a self-play environment consisting of a "Doctor Agent," a "Patient Agent," and a "Moderator" to scale learning across medical conditions [1]. Both AgentClinic and AMIE aim to move beyond single-turn interactions toward more complex, multi-turn diagnostic dialogues [1]. Furthermore, the "role-playing" framework introduced by CAMEL shares the underlying mechanism of using inception prompting to allow autonomous agents to complete tasks through collaborative conversation, similar to the interaction between the doctor and patient agents in AgentClinic [2]. The concept of specialized medical agents mimicking different clinical roles (e.g., radiologists or pathologists) is also discussed as a promising direction in existing medical LLM surveys [3].

**c) Unique Differences:**
The primary distinction of AgentClinic is its specific four-agent configuration, particularly the inclusion of a "measurement instrument" agent to facilitate the ordering and delivery of medical exams and multimodal data within the interactive loop. While existing systems like AMIE evaluate axes such as "Investigation Appropriate Recommended," the AMIE simulation primarily focuses on the dialogue between the doctor, patient, and a critic/moderator for training and refinement [1]. AgentClinic uniquely formalizes the "measurement instrument" as a distinct interacting entity to simulate the acquisition of objective medical data. Additionally, AgentClinic is positioned specifically as a benchmark to reveal the performance gap between static and sequential decision-making. It provides evidence that LLM diagnostic accuracy drops in interactive settings, contrasting with the typical focus of medical AI research on matching or exceeding human performance in static or fixed-input tasks [1] [4].

**d) Details of Unique Differences:**
As a System/Infrastructure innovation, AgentClinic addresses the reality that high scores on static medical benchmarks (such as the USMLE) do not necessarily translate to clinical competence in interactive, uncertain environments. Its design partitions the clinical encounter into four distinct roles, ensuring that the doctor agent must actively "pull" information from both the patient (subjective) and the measurement instrument (objective) agents. The system's utility is demonstrated through a benchmarking study of seven major LLMs, providing the insight that interactive diagnostic accuracy is a more difficult metric to satisfy than static accuracy. This validates the framework as a rigorous testing ground for AI safety and efficacy before real-world deployment, filling a gap in evaluative resources that rely on fixed datasets [1] [4].

### 2.2. Novelty Point 2: (Classification: Dataset/Benchmark)

**a) Claimed Novelty:**
Classification: Dataset/Benchmark.

Explanation: The novelty of AgentClinic lies in its creation of a comprehensive, multimodal benchmark designed to evaluate AI agents within simulated clinical environments. It structures cases by leveraging real-world electronic health records (EHRs) and multimodal medical journal challenges, covering nine distinct medical specialties and seven languages. This methodology moves beyond static question-answering by requiring agents to engage in interactive diagnostic tasks, such as requesting and interpreting physical examination findings and medical imaging. The benchmark addresses existing gaps in linguistic diversity and realism, providing a platform to study how language models generalize across specialized domains and language barriers. Its utility is supported by a clinical reader study where human doctors assessed the realism, empathy, and accuracy of the generated dialogues.

**b) Similarities:**
Several similarities exist regarding the use of simulated environments and evaluation frameworks. Like AgentClinic, the AMIE system utilizes a self-play based simulated learning environment to scale medical knowledge [5]. Similarly, the HELM framework advocates for a multi-metric perspective including accuracy, fairness, and bias, arguing that these should be evaluated in the contexts where models are deployed [6]. Furthermore, the use of LLM-based agents to simulate social interactions and decision-making is established in frameworks like CAMEL and MetaAgents, which utilize role-playing and multi-turn conversations [7] [8]. Existing work also identifies that LLM evaluators can suffer from inherent biases, such as self-preference, which can skew scoring [9] [10].

**c) Unique Differences:**
The primary distinction of AgentClinic is its specific focus on a multilingual and multimodal benchmark for interactive agents. While existing research like AMIE acknowledges the need for robustness in multilingual settings, AgentClinic explicitly incorporates seven different languages into its benchmark to test linguistic generalization [5]. Additionally, while other datasets are often limited in scope—focusing on specific conditions or populations—AgentClinic draws cases from multimodal medical journal challenges to ensure a diverse range of complex diagnostic tasks [17]. A significant functional difference is the requirement for agents to actively request and interpret multimodal inputs, such as image readings and physical exams, within the dialogue. This capability extends beyond the text-centric or static input evaluations seen in prior medical LLM benchmarks [5] [3].

**d) Details of Unique Differences:**
As a Dataset/Benchmark novelty, AgentClinic fills a critical gap in the diversity and interactive complexity of existing medical AI evaluation resources. While previous benchmarks often rely on static datasets or single-language transcripts, AgentClinic introduces a structured, multimodal, and multilingual framework that forces agents to navigate clinical uncertainty by requesting specific diagnostic information [5] [17]. This enables new research into the ability of LLMs to overcome linguistic barriers and manage multi-step diagnostic reasoning where textual dialogue must be integrated with requested visual or physical data. The system's practicality is demonstrated through its use of real-world EHR structures and its validation via a clinical reader study, ensuring the benchmark reflects the realism required for actual medical practice.

### 2.3. Novelty Point 3: (Classification: Empirical/Analytical)

**a) Claimed Novelty:**
Classification: Empirical/Analytical.

Explanation: This novelty point presents an empirical and analytical study of how simulated cognitive and implicit biases impact medical interactions within an AI-driven clinical environment. The methodology involves the systematic injection of 24 distinct cognitive and implicit biases into the system prompts of both patient and doctor agents. The central finding is a decoupling of technical performance and interpersonal outcomes: even when a model's diagnostic accuracy remains stable despite these perturbations, the presence of bias leads to a sharp decline in patient-centric metrics. Specifically, simulated patients reported significantly lower confidence in the provider, a reduced likelihood of adhering to treatment plans, and a decreased willingness to engage in follow-up consultations. This evidence challenges the reliance on diagnostic accuracy as the primary benchmark for medical AI, highlighting that interpersonal factors and bias-related degradation are critical for evaluating healthcare delivery.

**b) Similarities:**
The multi-metric approach to evaluation shares similarities with existing research. For instance, the AMIE system is evaluated not only on diagnostic accuracy but also on communication quality, empathy, and trust using a simulated Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE) framework [11]. The HELM framework also advocates for evaluating metrics like bias and toxicity across the same scenarios used to measure accuracy [6]. Furthermore, Galactica uses a curated scientific corpus to avoid mirroring the misinformation and stereotypes found in general-purpose models [12].

**c) Unique Differences:**
The primary distinction is the empirical focus on the specific relationship between injected clinical biases and the degradation of patient-centric outcomes (trust and compliance) relative to stable diagnostic accuracy. While AMIE measures communication and trust, it focuses on comparing AI performance against human physicians in a randomized, double-blind crossover study rather than systematically perturbing agents with a library of 24 specific cognitive and implicit biases to observe isolated effects [11]. Unlike the HELM framework [6] or Galactica [12], this novelty describes a dynamic analytical finding: that bias acts as a catalyst for losing patient trust and compliance even when the "correct" medical answer is reached. This specifically addresses the "accuracy-trust gap" in a way that prior benchmarks, which largely focus on accuracy or static bias detection, do not explore.

**d) Details of Unique Differences:**
The central insight is that diagnostic accuracy is an insufficient proxy for effective healthcare delivery because interpersonal metrics like trust and compliance can collapse due to implicit biases even when accuracy remains high. This finding challenges the belief that a "correct" diagnosis is the most vital metric for medical AI. The validity of this insight is supported by an experimental design that uses system prompt engineering to inject 24 specific biases into doctor and patient agents, allowing for a controlled comparison between objective diagnostic correctness and subjective quantitative rating scales. By demonstrating this divergence, the analysis highlights a critical requirement for medical AI: the need for alignment that preserves the patient-provider relationship, not just the accuracy of the medical output.

### 2.4. Novelty Point 4: (Classification: Empirical/Analytical)

**a) Claimed Novelty:**
Classification: Empirical/Analytical.

Explanation: This novelty point identifies a significant empirical finding regarding the non-uniform impact of tool-augmented reasoning—specifically "persistent experiential learning notebooks"—on the clinical diagnostic performance of various LLMs. The methodology involves providing an agent with an editable notebook that persists across sequential patient cases, allowing the model to record and accumulate experiential knowledge. The central discovery is that the utility of such tools is highly model-dependent: while models like Claude 3.5 reach peak performance and others like Llama 3 exhibit massive relative accuracy gains (up to 92%), others, specifically GPT-3.5, suffer performance degradation. This finding is supported by an ablation study across six different agent tools and multiple LLM backbones, challenging the assumption that external tools like adaptive retrieval or reflection modules provide a universal benefit to model reasoning.

**b) Similarities:**
The concept of using a persistent memory structure to accumulate knowledge is explored in several works. The ExpeL agent gathers experiences and extracts knowledge in natural language to inform future decisions [13]. Similarly, the "Generative Agents" architecture utilizes a "memory stream" to maintain a record of experiences, which are synthesized into higher-level "reflections" [14]. In the domain of embodied agents, VOYAGER employs an "ever-growing skill library" of executable code [15]. Furthermore, the use of iterative reasoning chains to improve diagnostic accuracy is shared with AMIE, which employs a three-step chain-of-reasoning [11].

**c) Unique Differences:**
The primary distinction is the focus on the divergent performance outcomes across different LLM backbones when using identical tool-augmented reasoning structures. While works like ExpeL and Generative Agents generally report consistent improvements [13] [14], this novelty point explicitly identifies a "performance drop" in specific models like GPT-3.5 when tools are introduced. Unlike ToolLLM, which focuses on the difficulty of mastering thousands of APIs [16], this analysis isolates the "persistent notebook" as a specific variable within a clinical agentic loop to measure relative accuracy shifts. It provides a comparative benchmark that challenges the assumption of uniform tool benefit.

**d) Details of Unique Differences:**
The central insight is that tool-augmented reasoning does not act as a universal performance booster but instead interacts with a model's underlying architecture in ways that can either enhance or hinder clinical reasoning. This challenges the belief that providing more "reasoning scaffolding" will inherently improve outcomes. The validity of this insight is supported by a comprehensive ablation study revealing that while a model like Llama 3 can overcome sequential decision-making deficits, the complexity of managing these tools causes GPT-3.5's performance to degenerate. This suggests that the "cognitive overhead" or instruction-following limitations of certain models may make tool integration counterproductive, emphasizing the need for model-specific tool optimization.

### 2.5. Novelty Point 5: (Classification: Empirical/Analytical)

**a) Claimed Novelty:**
Classification: Empirical/Analytical.

Explanation: This novelty point presents an empirical finding that an LLM's performance on static medical question-answering (QA) benchmarks, such as MedQA or USMLE-style exams, is a poor predictor of its diagnostic accuracy in dynamic, interactive clinical simulations. The methodology involves a comparative analysis of seven major language models, demonstrating that solving medical problems in a dialogue-driven, sequential decision-making environment is significantly more complex than selecting answers in static multiple-choice formats. Evidence shows that diagnostic accuracies in interactive settings drop to a fraction of the scores achieved on static benchmarks, with only a weak correlation between the two metrics. This finding challenges the reliance on static benchmarks to determine if an AI is "clinically ready," arguing that interactive environments are essential for a true assessment of medical capability.

**b) Similarities:**
The challenge of transitioning from static medical knowledge to interactive dialogue is acknowledged in research noting that medical diagnostic dialogue is a difficult use case due to the complexity of interactive information gathering [1]. Existing work also recognizes that while LLMs are accurate in single-turn medical QA, their conversational capabilities for history-taking require more rigorous examination [1]. Prior studies have observed that AI models interpreting fixed clinical inputs perform differently than those required to actively acquire information through conversation [1]. Research has also pointed out that high-performing models on benchmarks like MedQA-USMLE still face limitations in real-world reasoning [4]. Earlier attempts to create simulated medical conversations also showed that accuracy in answer selection remained limited [17].

**c) Unique Differences:**
The primary distinction is the empirical focus on the lack of correlation between static and interactive performance across seven major LLMs. While research like the AMIE study focuses on optimizing a specific model for dialogue, this novelty point provides a systemic critique of static benchmarks by quantifying their failure to predict interactive success [1]. It introduces a specific analytical finding: that diagnostic accuracy falls to a "fraction" of static scores and is poorly correlated across multiple architectures. Unlike previous works that aim to improve benchmark scores, this analysis investigates how constraints on interaction time and inference steps further decouple static knowledge from interactive diagnostic utility [4] [1].

**d) Details of Unique Differences:**
The central finding is the identification of a significant "capability gap" where high performance on medical board exams does not translate into effective clinical dialogue. This challenges the belief that static benchmarks are a valid proxy for clinical readiness [4] [1]. This insight is supported by an experimental design testing seven distinct LLMs on both standard medical QA datasets and a new interactive benchmark. The validity of this finding is further established by demonstrating that when dialogue turns or inference steps are limited, the models' ability to reach an accurate diagnosis collapses, even if their underlying medical knowledge remains high. This suggests that the procedural skill of information acquisition is a distinct capability from the retrieval of medical facts, necessitating a shift in how medical AI performance is evaluated [1].

## 3. Novelty Summary

This paper demonstrates an integration-driven approach and significant empirical breakthroughs by shifting the evaluation of medical AI from static question-answering to dynamic, interactive clinical simulations. By combining a novel multi-agent architecture with a diverse, multimodal dataset and rigorous behavioral analyses, the work systematically exposes the limitations of current evaluation paradigms. It challenges prevailing assumptions by proving that static medical knowledge does not equate to interactive clinical competence, while highlighting critical vulnerabilities regarding patient trust and the non-uniform impact of tool-augmented reasoning.

*   **Genuine Innovations:** The most significant innovation is the formalization of a four-agent clinical simulation that introduces a "measurement instrument" agent alongside the doctor, patient, and moderator. This forces the primary diagnostic agent to actively request and interpret objective multimodal data across diverse languages and specialties, rigorously testing sequential decision-making under uncertainty. Another major contribution is the empirical discovery of the "accuracy-trust gap." By systematically injecting 24 specific biases, the authors demonstrate that interpersonal metrics like patient trust and compliance can severely degrade even when diagnostic accuracy remains stable. Furthermore, the paper provides novel analytical insights into model behaviors, quantifying that tool-augmented reasoning yields divergent, model-dependent results. Finally, it systemically proves that high performance on static medical exams is a poor, weakly correlated predictor of interactive diagnostic success.

*   **Aspects Lacking Originality:** The foundational use of multi-agent role-playing and standard clinical evaluation rubrics (assessing realism, accuracy, and empathy) represents a **Standard Practice** in prior simulated medical environments. *Improvement suggestion:* The authors could develop new, interaction-specific evaluation metrics tailored to the unique dynamics of the measurement instrument. Additionally, the implementation of persistent memory structures is a **Direct Repetition** of established agentic memory architectures. *Improvement suggestion:* Future iterations should propose a novel memory mechanism specifically optimized for clinical reasoning and medical evidence weighting.

**Final One-line Summary:**  
**3 – Good / Significant:** This work achieves substantial progress by introducing a well-motivated interactive evaluation framework and presenting new empirical insights that challenge the community's reliance on static benchmarks and universal tool benefits in medical AI.
